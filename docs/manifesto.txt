lit — A Field Theory of Lighting
=================================

Lighting programming should be declarative, not imperative.

Today's consoles are imperative:
  "Fixture 1 → Pan 45°. Fixture 2 → Cyan. Run effect X. Go."

lit is declarative:
  "There is a vortex above the drummer.
   Colour is a function of distance to that vortex.
   All beams follow the flow.
   Preserve bilateral symmetry."

The engine decides what every fixture must do to satisfy those declarations.

This is a new paradigm. It rests on three concepts.

1. Space
--------
The stage is a continuous 3D coordinate system. Every fixture, every beam,
every scenic element exists in this space. The engine does not think in
terms of fixture IDs, DMX addresses, or channel numbers. It thinks in
terms of positions and orientations in space.

2. Fields
---------
A field is a function that answers the question:
  "What is happening at this point in space, at this moment in time?"

A scalar field returns one value (intensity, zoom, hue).
A vector field returns a direction or velocity (beam orientation, flow).
A target field returns a point in space (look‑at target).

Fields can be composed, added, multiplied, masked, and transformed.
Symmetry is a field operator: it mirrors the field, not the fixtures.
A sweep is a moving window in a scalar field.
A circle is a rotating vector field.

Fields are defined by the designer using a visual graph. That graph is a
mathematical definition of the spatial environment. It does not describe
what fixtures do; it describes what space does.

3. Entities
-----------
A fixture is a probe. It occupies a position in space and has a set of
capabilities (dimmer, pan/tilt, colour, gobo, etc.).

Every frame, the fixture asks the field engine:
  "What is the intensity field at my position?"
  "What is the orientation field at my position?"
  "What is the colour field at my position?"

The fixture receives generic, semantic answers: a brightness, a direction,
an HSL colour. It then uses its own profile to convert those answers into
physical DMX values. A dimmer‑only fixture ignores orientation. An RGBW
fixture converts HSL to its emitter mix. A moving head solves inverse
kinematics to point its beam in the requested direction.

The fixture does not know about effects. It only knows about space.

The pipeline
------------
  1. Fields are defined and composed (the Builder).
  2. The field graph is baked into a single continuous function.
  3. At runtime, the function is evaluated on the GPU — one dispatch
     produces a spatial texture.
  4. Every fixture samples the texture at its world position.
  5. The resulting semantic values are mapped to DMX by fixture profiles.
  6. Parameters (speed, colour, intensity, symmetry) are live‑tweakable
     with zero latency — they are just uniforms to the GPU function.

Why this matters
----------------
- The rig is replaceable. The field does not change when fixtures change.
  Add ten more fixtures, and they join the field automatically.
- Symmetry is a coordinate transform, not a cue‑list duplication.
- Colour is a spatial property, not a per‑fixture palette choice.
- Effects are emergent. A "circle" is just a rotating field. A "chase" is
  a moving wave.
- The designer composes space, not commands.

lit is not a node‑graph editor, a shader toolkit, or a better MA2.
It is a field engine for light. The rest is implementation.
